Introduction

 

The topic we chose to explore is the reduction of churning through the use of data mining for marketing plan development. The problem we addressed is that of a telecommunications company looking for ways to reduce customer churning by developing a targeted marketing program. The company wishes to do this by identifying combinations of voice mail and/or international calling plan packages that could effectively be offered to the various customer groups. The customer groups are divided into calling time habits such as day, evening, and night. Also, there are many customers that make international calls, and those customers may represent a distinct group for targeting new plan packages. With pattern information gained from mining of call data, the firm hopes to identify which plan-customer group pairs have the lowest churn rates. Identification of such patterns should enable the firm to optimize its marketing strategy for churn reduction.



Data Description

The churn dataset deals with telecommunications customers and the data pertinent to the telephone calls they make. Customers have a choice of carriers, or companies providing them with telephone service. When these customers change carriers they are said to churn which results in a loss of revenue for the previous carrier. Methodology Followed

The analysis was basically done to identify possible groups of people on the basis of causes that may lead to churning. To see the relationship between the people who churn and other factors like the amount of international calls they make, we ran scatter and splat analysis with all possible combinations Any initial pattern identified in the beginning (scatter analysis) was further analyzed by checking for that same pattern when run with several similar combinations. Later decision tree analysis and evidence classifier were utilized to group people in to several possible domains with certain common traits.Causes for the churning were later extracted from the visualizations done in mineset and are presented in our conclusions.

The values in the column under the question mark are the cost from predicting "unknown".


For those values in (1), if we predict a customer will not churn, and we are correct, we neither win or lose (represented by zero). If we predict a customer will not churn (and therefore fail to send them any incentives), and they do churn, we incur a loss of 10. If we incorrectly predict a customer will churn, and they do not, we lose three, representing the cost of sending a mailing unnecessarily. If our mailing program works and we retain some of the customers who would have churned, we gain 10 (represented by minus 10).

In (2), we reduce the value of our mailing cost and increase the value of retaining a customer.

From the two ROI curves, we can see that the expected performance if we take a random sample of the population and send them mail is distinctly different, because the costs of mailing are different. If the mailing cost is small enough (for example, companies now can send email to customers with little cost), then it is probably a good idea to send mails to customers as more as possible since the random ROI curve is a monotonically increasing line.

The points of optimum return on investment, are represented by the knee of the curve, at 1488 (12.6% of the population) and 3,513 (30% of the population) respectively, because the gain from retaining customers doubles. If the gain of retaining a customer is much larger comparing with the mailing cost and the mailing program works, so the company can also enlarge the scope of its mailing program.

    Conclusions and Recommendations

    After a thorough review of the available data in the Churn dataset, our group arrived at the following conclusions, from which we will make our recommendation.


    Conclusions

    1. Voice-mail plans: People with a voice-mail plan do not churn in 92% of all cases. This finding seems to be fairly independent of other data. Without additional information that can help determine why this group does not churn, we believe the company should make voice-mail standard in all plans.
    2. Account length: This information showed that most people tend to stay with the company about 100 months before churning. The supporting data was insufficient to develop any meaningful or discernable patterns in relation to other variables. Although the data is inconclusive, this category helps define a terminal length to which the company should aspire to entice the churners to reach.
    3. Customer Service Calls: If the number of customer service calls is greater than 3 the customers begin to churn. When the number of service calls per customer reaches 8, the level of dissatisfaction is so high that nearly all customers leave the company.
    4. Total Calls: Those customers that churn can also be identified to make between 60 and 120 calls per month. Since it is not logical to try and get customers to make fewer calls, we concentrated our efforts on increasing the number of calls above 120. The resultant model was used to generate a lift curve. Results from this lift curve were somewhat disappointing, as it showed an increase in return of only 8%.
    5. International Calls: Those customers making international calls were the most susceptible to churning. This assumes a relation to the high cost of international calling rates relative to all other rates. Those individuals making more than 12.5 minutes of international calls were very likely to churn.
    6. Daytime Calls: Persons making most of their calls during daytime hours are very likely to churn. This is assumed to result from the same issue of high rates that also affects international calls.


    Recommendation

    It is our belief that the company should make voice-mail a standard part of all calling plans. Second, more information is needed for a thorough analysis of calling rates so that the company can possibly offer reduced rate plans for the daytime and/or international customers. Third, the company needs to target improvements to customer service as a way to reduce churning. This last item can be from improvements in the customer service department as well as from mailings to customers already with three complaints. The customers with three complaints should be questioned to find out the primary reasons for their telecommunication difficulties.
